# Beyond Pixels: Architecting MLLMs for Deep AEC Document Understanding

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have demonstrated impressive capabilities across various domains, yet their application to complex visual documents, particularly within the Architecture, Engineering, and Construction (AEC) sector, reveals significant limitations. Current MLLMs often struggle to move beyond superficial pixel-level analysis, failing to capture the intricate hierarchical structures, precise geometric relationships, and domain-specific semantics essential for interpreting technical drawings, schematics, and plans. This deficiency directly impedes the development of truly intelligent automation for critical AEC workflows. The overarching thesis is that robust, geometrically-aware, and hierarchically structured understanding, coupled with resilience to real-world visual degradations, is not merely an enhancement but a fundamental prerequisite for architecting next-generation AEC foundation models capable of deep 2D document intelligence.

## The Imperative for Deep Visual-Geometric-Semantic Understanding in AEC Documents

The AEC industry relies heavily on 2D documents—CAD drawings, floor plans, electrical schematics, bills of materials, and specifications—which are rich in visual, geometric, and semantic information. Traditional MLLMs, while adept at general image understanding and text generation, often falter when confronted with the highly structured, symbolic, and often abstract visual language of AEC documents. They may identify individual components but fail to grasp their nested relationships, precise spatial arrangements, or the implicit design intent conveyed through a hierarchy of assemblies and sub-assemblies. This gap necessitates a paradigm shift: MLLMs must evolve to treat geometry and hierarchy as first-class citizens in their representational foundation, rather than as mere auxiliary signals. Furthermore, real-world AEC documents frequently suffer from visual degradations due to scanning, printing, or storage conditions, demanding models that are robust to imperfect inputs.

## COCOTree's Automated Pipeline for Open Tree-Structured Visual Decomposition

To address the challenge of hierarchically interpreting visual components, COCOTree introduces a novel, fully automated pipeline for open tree-structured visual decomposition [E3, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22068]. This mechanism synergizes the semantic reasoning capabilities of Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) with the precise geometric grounding provided by the Segment Anything Model (SAM). This combination effectively overcomes the prohibitive cognitive and physical bottlenecks associated with manual annotation of complex hierarchical structures [E3]. The pipeline enables the generation of detailed annotations that capture nested components and their relationships, moving beyond flat object detection.

Leveraging this automated pipeline, the COCOTree benchmark was constructed, a massive-scale dataset featuring over 21,000 images and 1.8 million structural nodes [E1, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22068]. This benchmark embraces an open-vocabulary space with over 3,500 unique labels, designed to capture the long-tail distribution of complex physical assemblies [E1]. To standardize evaluation for this new paradigm, the Open Tree Quality (OTQ) metric was proposed, which jointly assesses mask precision, label accuracy, and structural consistency [E4, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22068]. Rigorous human evaluation confirmed that the generated annotations demonstrate strong alignment with human structural judgment, validating the quality and utility of the dataset [E5, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22068].

For AEC applications, COCOTree's approach has direct implications. The ability to automatically decompose images into hierarchical trees of visual components with unconstrained granularity is critical for interpreting complex AEC documents like CAD drawings, assembly instructions, and schematics. This allows MLLMs to understand not just individual lines or symbols, but how they group into components, sub-assemblies, and ultimately, a complete design. This moves AEC document intelligence beyond simple object recognition to a deeper comprehension of design intent and functional relationships. An open hypothesis for AEC is validating whether this mechanism remains useful under the specific distributions and evaluation constraints of highly specialized AEC document types, which often feature abstract symbols and multi-layered conventions not typically found in general image datasets. Adapting the LVLM component through domain-specific fine-tuning or specialized prompt engineering would likely be necessary.

## GeoWeaver's Token-Adaptive Geometric Evidence Allocation for Spatial Reasoning

Complementing hierarchical decomposition, GeoWeaver introduces a pre-reasoning geometric grounding framework that fundamentally treats geometry as a representational prerequisite for robust spatio-temporal reasoning in MLLMs [E6, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558; E9, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558]. This mechanism departs from approaches that treat geometric cues as a shared signal across all visual tokens or as a late-fusion auxiliary signal. Instead, GeoWeaver integrates geometry at a foundational level, shaping the representational basis upon which MLLMs perform reasoning [E9].

The core of GeoWeaver involves constructing a multi-level geometry bank from a frozen geometry encoder. This bank allows for token-adaptive geometric evidence allocation, enabling each visual token to retrieve the most relevant geometric abstractions pertinent to its spatial role [E10, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558]. The selected geometric evidence is then incorporated into visual tokens via a residual grounding operation *before* language modeling, yielding geometry-grounded representations for downstream reasoning [E10]. Extensive evaluations on spatial reasoning benchmarks demonstrate that GeoWeaver consistently enhances geometry-aware reasoning while retaining general multimodal capabilities [E7, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558].

This fine-grained, representational prerequisite for geometry is critically important for achieving precise spatial intelligence in AEC applications. In AEC, accurate physical relationships, dimensions, clearances, and relative positions within complex drawings are paramount for ensuring design integrity, constructability, and operational efficiency. For instance, understanding if a pipe segment is *parallel to* a wall, *perpendicular to* another pipe, or *within* a specific clearance zone requires more than just identifying the objects; it demands precise geometric comprehension. GeoWeaver's approach offers a plausible transfer to AEC by enabling MLLMs to deeply understand and interpret these exact spatial relationships, moving beyond mere visual recognition to true geometric comprehension. However, the integration of such a pre-reasoning framework, requiring a dedicated geometry encoder and token-adaptive allocation, may introduce additional computational overhead or architectural complexity, potentially impacting inference speed and deployment costs in real-time AEC workflows.

## ReceiptBench and Metric-Aware GRPO for Structurally Consistent Information Extraction

Beyond visual and geometric understanding, Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) face significant challenges in real-world document understanding due to limitations in existing benchmarks regarding scale, realism, semantic granularity, and the diversity of document types [E12, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22413]. To bridge this gap, ReceiptBench introduces a large-scale, human-annotated benchmark comprising 10,000 diverse receipts [E15, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22413]. This benchmark organizes information extraction into four hierarchical sub-tasks: (1) Basic Perception for raw text spotting, (2) Format Normalization for adhering to standardization instructions, (3) Semantic Reasoning for inferring implicit attributes from context, and (4) Structure Parsing for handling nested line items [E15]. This hierarchical task breakdown is crucial for capturing the complexity of real-world documents.

Complementing this benchmark, a two-stage training framework is proposed, incorporating Metric-Aware Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) [E11, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22413]. This mechanism translates rigorous evaluation constraints, such as those for structural consistency, into reinforcement learning signals, thereby enhancing the model's ability to produce structurally sound outputs [E11]. Extensive experiments demonstrate that this method yields state-of-the-art performance, surpassing leading proprietary models on complex reasoning tasks [E14, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22413]. The datasets and code for ReceiptBench are publicly released, fostering further research [E13, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22413].

For Architecture, Engineering, and Construction (AEC), ReceiptBench's emphasis on hierarchical parsing and structural consistency has direct implications. It offers a highly transferable approach for extracting structured data from diverse AEC documents, such as bills of materials, equipment schedules, project specifications, or inspection reports, where maintaining the integrity of nested components, itemized lists, and tabular data is paramount. For example, extracting quantities and specifications from a bill of materials requires not just recognizing individual text fields but understanding their grouping, hierarchical relationships (e.g., sub-assemblies), and adherence to specific formatting rules. The GRPO framework's ability to enforce structural consistency is vital for generating reliable, machine-readable data from these documents. However, while effective for receipts, adapting the specific hierarchical sub-tasks and metric-aware optimization to the highly varied and domain-specific structures found across diverse AEC document types (e.g., different table layouts, nested lists in specifications) may require substantial re-definition of tasks and metrics, posing a challenge for broad adoption without significant domain-specific fine-tuning and validation.

## SpaceDG-Bench: Quantifying MLLM Robustness to Visual Degradation in Spatial Understanding

Even with advancements in hierarchical and geometric understanding, Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) exhibit significant fragility in spatial understanding when confronted with real-world visual degradations [E16, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22536]. Despite rapid progress in spatial intelligence under pristine conditions, existing benchmarks largely overlook common degradations like motion blur, low light, adverse weather, lens distortion, and compression artifacts that frequently occur in real-world deployments [E16]. This raises a fundamental question about the robustness of MLLMs in practical scenarios.

To address this, SpaceDG introduces the first large-scale dataset specifically designed for degradation-aware spatial understanding [E17, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22536]. The dataset is constructed using a physically grounded degradation synthesis engine that embeds the degradation formation process into 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) rendering. This enables realistic simulation of nine distinct degradation types, resulting in approximately 1 million QA pairs from nearly 1,000 indoor scenes [E19, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22536]. Complementing this, SpaceDG-Bench provides a human-verified benchmark with 1,102 questions spanning 11 reasoning categories and 9 visual degradation types, yielding over 10,000 VQA instances [E20, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22536]. Evaluations of multiple open- and closed-source MLLMs reveal that visual degradations consistently and substantially impair spatial reasoning, exposing a critical robustness gap [E16].

Crucially, the research demonstrates that finetuning MLLMs on SpaceDG markedly improves their degradation robustness, even enabling them to surpass human performance under degraded conditions without any performance drop on clean images [E18, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22536]. This highlights the significant promise of degradation-aware training for robust spatial intelligence. For AEC, this is a direct implication, as scanned blueprints, faded historical drawings, or low-quality photographs of construction sites are common. MLLMs must be resilient to these imperfections to be truly useful. As a complementary approach, supporting research on self-evolving image restoration systems like EvoIR-Agent can achieve a significant lead in full reference metrics and yield a remarkable Pareto-optimal balance between performance and efficiency in restoring degraded images [E23, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22208]. However, integrating such extensive datasets and finetuning processes may introduce substantial computational overhead during training and potentially increase inference latency if restoration is performed as a pre-processing step. Furthermore, some training-free restoration methods, while compatible with new tools, can incur trial-and-error overhead due to naive experience, potentially impacting real-time application in dynamic AEC environments [E24, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22208].

## Synergies and Tradeoffs: Weaving Hierarchies, Geometry, and Robustness for AEC

The integration of hierarchical decomposition (COCOTree), precise geometric grounding (GeoWeaver), robust structured extraction (ReceiptBench), and resilience to visual degradation (SpaceDG-Bench) presents a complex but essential engineering challenge for AEC foundation models. These capabilities are deeply interconnected: accurate geometric understanding often relies on correctly identified hierarchical components, and both must be robust to the imperfect visual inputs common in real-world AEC documents.

For instance, interpreting a floor plan requires not only segmenting rooms and furniture (hierarchy, COCOTree) but also understanding their precise dimensions and relative positions (geometry, GeoWeaver). If the scanned plan is blurry or distorted (degradation, SpaceDG-Bench), both hierarchical and geometric understanding will suffer. Similarly, extracting data from a bill of materials (structured extraction, ReceiptBench) might involve recognizing tables and nested line items, which are themselves visual components with implicit hierarchical and spatial relationships.

Engineering challenges arise in combining these specialized models. A modular architecture, where a degradation-aware pre-processing step (potentially leveraging EvoIR-Agent for efficient image restoration [E23, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22208]) feeds into a geometrically-grounded, hierarchically-aware MLLM, seems plausible. However, this introduces potential tradeoffs. Inference latency is a major concern for interactive AEC workflows. Each additional processing step, whether for image restoration, geometric grounding, or hierarchical decomposition, adds to the overall computation time. For example, while FastTab demonstrates competitive table structure recovery performance at low-latency inference [E21, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22422], extending such optimized, accurate methods across the diverse and often more complex visual structures of AEC documents, especially when also incorporating resilience to visual degradation, is an open challenge.

The design of benchmarks and validation plans for these integrated systems must also be holistic. An adoption test for an AEC foundation model would need to evaluate not just the accuracy of individual tasks (e.g., object detection, text extraction) but the end-to-end performance on complex reasoning tasks that span hierarchical, geometric, and semantic understanding, under various degradation conditions. This requires a new generation of AEC-specific benchmarks that combine the rigor of SpaceDG-Bench's degradation simulation with the hierarchical and structural complexity of COCOTree and ReceiptBench.

## Engineering Impact: Towards AEC Foundation Models for Intelligent 2D Document Workflows

The advancements in hierarchical visual decomposition, precise geometric grounding, robust structured information extraction, and resilience to visual degradation collectively offer a transformative path towards truly intelligent 2D document workflows within the AEC industry. For Autodesk, these research directions are directly actionable for enhancing existing products and developing next-generation foundation models.

**Intelligent CAD Drawing Interpretation:** Leveraging COCOTree's automated pipeline, MLLMs can move beyond simple object recognition in CAD drawings to understand the nested hierarchy of components, assemblies, and sub-assemblies [E1, E3, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22068]. Combined with GeoWeaver's precise geometric grounding [E6, E7, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558], this enables MLLMs to interpret design intent, validate spatial relationships (e.g., clearances, alignments), and even identify potential design conflicts directly from visual inputs. This could power automated design review, clash detection, and intelligent search within vast archives of drawings.

**Automated Bill of Materials (BOM) and Schedule Extraction:** ReceiptBench's framework for structurally consistent information extraction [E11, E15, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22413] is directly transferable to extracting structured data from AEC documents like BOMs, equipment schedules, and specifications. This would automate the tedious and error-prone process of manually transcribing data, ensuring high structural consistency and accuracy for downstream applications like procurement, cost estimation, and project management.

**Robust Plan Analysis from Scanned Documents:** The insights from SpaceDG-Bench [E16, E18, https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22536] are critical for handling the pervasive issue of degraded documents in AEC. By incorporating degradation-aware training, MLLMs can maintain high performance even when analyzing scanned blueprints, faded historical drawings, or low-quality site photos. This ensures that valuable information from legacy documents remains accessible and interpretable, enhancing data continuity across project lifecycles.

**Enhancing Existing 2D Document Workflows:** These advanced MLLM capabilities can be integrated into existing Autodesk platforms to provide intelligent assistance for architects, engineers, and construction professionals. This includes smart annotation suggestions, automated quantity take-offs, intelligent search and retrieval of relevant document sections, and cross-referencing information across different document types. The engineering challenge lies in optimizing these integrated systems for low-latency inference and ensuring their robustness across the highly diverse and specialized visual language of AEC documents. The potential for these advancements to unlock new levels of automation and insight in AEC workflows is immense, paving the way for more efficient, accurate, and intelligent project delivery.
