# Robust MLLMs for AEC: Grounding, Structure, and Degradation Resilience in Document Intelligence

## The AEC Document Intelligence Imperative: Why MLLMs Need More Than Just Vision

The Architecture, Engineering, and Construction (AEC) industry relies heavily on a vast array of 2D documents, from intricate blueprints and detailed schedules to complex forms and specifications. Extracting actionable intelligence from these documents is paramount for project success, but it presents unique challenges for AI. AEC documents are often noisy, scanned from physical copies, contain critical spatial relationships, and demand precise structural understanding. Current Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs), while powerful, often fall short in these specific areas. To be truly effective in AEC, MLLMs must evolve to incorporate robust geometric grounding, comprehend intricate structural hierarchies, and maintain performance despite the visual degradations common in real-world scans. This post explores recent advancements that directly address these critical needs, paving the way for more intelligent and reliable AEC document processing.

## ReceiptBench and Metric-Aware GRPO: Architecting Structural Consistency in Document MLLMs

A fundamental challenge for MLLMs in document intelligence is the accurate extraction of structured information, particularly from real-world documents that often deviate from pristine digital formats. Existing MLLM benchmarks for document understanding have suffered from critical limitations, including insufficient scale, lack of realism, limited semantic granularity, and a failure to cover diverse document types [E2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22413). To address this, ReceiptBench was introduced as a large-scale, human-annotated benchmark comprising 10,000 diverse receipts [E5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22413). This benchmark meticulously organizes information extraction into four hierarchical sub-tasks: Basic Perception for raw text spotting, Format Normalization for adhering to standardization instructions, Semantic Reasoning for inferring implicit attributes from context, and Structure Parsing for handling nested line items [E5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22413).

To enhance structural consistency, a critical requirement for reliable document parsing, the researchers propose a two-stage training framework incorporating Metric-Aware Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) [E1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22413). This innovative mechanism translates rigorous evaluation constraints directly into reinforcement learning signals, guiding the MLLM to produce outputs that strictly adhere to desired structural formats and relationships [E1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22413). Extensive experiments demonstrate that this method achieves state-of-the-art performance, notably surpassing leading proprietary models on complex reasoning tasks [E4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22413).

For AEC, where documents like schedules, bills of materials, and standardized forms demand precise structural understanding and adherence to specific, often nested, formats, GRPO's ability to enforce structural consistency presents a direct and highly valuable implication. This approach could significantly reduce errors in automated data extraction from critical project documentation, such as ensuring that line items in a bill of quantities are correctly associated with their respective categories and sub-categories. While the benchmark focuses on receipts, the underlying mechanism of translating structural evaluation constraints into RL signals is plausibly transferable to AEC document distributions. An open hypothesis remains to validate its utility under the unique structural and semantic constraints of AEC documents. From an engineering perspective, adopting ReceiptBench's hierarchical sub-tasks offers a robust framework for designing internal validation benchmarks, while GRPO provides a clear path to architecting models with improved structural consistency, a key failure mode in current document parsing systems.

## GeoWeaver's Token-Adaptive Geometric Grounding: A Prerequisite for Spatial Reasoning

Beyond merely understanding text and its structure, AEC documents, particularly blueprints and technical drawings, demand a profound grasp of spatial relationships. Traditional multimodal models often incorporate geometric information as a shared, late-fusion auxiliary signal, which limits their inherent spatio-temporal reasoning capabilities [E11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558). This approach overlooks the critical nuance that different visual tokens require distinct geometric evidence depending on their specific spatial roles [E14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558). GeoWeaver addresses this limitation by introducing a pre-reasoning geometric grounding framework that treats geometry not as an afterthought, but as a fundamental representational prerequisite for spatio-temporal reasoning [E11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558), [E14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558).

The core mechanism of GeoWeaver involves constructing a multi-level geometry bank from a frozen geometry encoder [E15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558). This bank allows for token-adaptive geometric evidence allocation, where each visual token can retrieve the most relevant geometric abstractions tailored to its context before any scene reasoning commences [E15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558). The selected geometric evidence is then incorporated into the visual tokens via a residual grounding operation prior to language modeling, thereby yielding geometry-grounded representations that form a more robust foundation for downstream reasoning [E14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558). Extensive evaluations on spatial reasoning benchmarks demonstrate that GeoWeaver consistently enhances geometry-aware reasoning while retaining general multimodal capabilities [E12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558).

For AEC, GeoWeaver's approach has direct implications for interpreting complex layouts, relative positions, and dimensional constraints in blueprints and other technical drawings. An MLLM equipped with token-adaptive geometric grounding could more accurately identify and reason about the spatial relationships between building components, understand clearances, and detect potential clashes, moving beyond simple object detection to true spatial intelligence. This represents a plausible transfer of a foundational architectural improvement. Integrating such a pre-reasoning step into existing MLLM pipelines would require careful architectural consideration, potentially impacting inference latency and computational cost, but the enhanced accuracy in spatial reasoning could justify these tradeoffs for high-value AEC tasks. The availability of source code and models [E13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558) facilitates practical integration and validation.

## SpaceDG: Quantifying and Mitigating Spatial Reasoning Degradation in Real-World Scans

Even with sophisticated geometric grounding, the real-world conditions under which AEC documents are captured often introduce visual degradations that can severely impair MLLM performance. Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have shown rapid progress in spatial intelligence, but existing spatial reasoning benchmarks largely assume pristine visual inputs, overlooking common degradations like motion blur, low light, adverse weather, lens distortion, and compression artifacts [E16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22536). This oversight exposes a critical robustness gap when MLLMs are deployed in practical, noisy environments.

To address this, SpaceDG introduces the first large-scale dataset specifically designed for degradation-aware spatial understanding [E17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22536). Its mechanism relies on a physically grounded degradation synthesis engine that embeds the degradation formation process into 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) rendering, enabling realistic simulation of nine distinct degradation types [E17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22536). The resulting dataset comprises approximately 1 million QA pairs derived from nearly 1,000 indoor scenes [E19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22536). This dataset underpins SpaceDG-Bench, a human-verified benchmark featuring 1,102 questions spanning 11 reasoning categories and 9 visual degradation types, yielding over 10,000 Visual Question Answering (VQA) instances [E20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22536).

Evaluations of multiple open- and closed-source MLLMs on SpaceDG-Bench reveal that visual degradations consistently and substantially impair spatial reasoning, highlighting a critical robustness deficiency [E16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22536). Crucially, the research demonstrates that finetuning MLLMs on SpaceDG markedly improves degradation robustness, enabling models to even surpass human performance under degraded conditions without any performance drop on clean images [E18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22536). This finding has direct and significant implications for AEC document intelligence. Scans and photographs of blueprints, site plans, and forms frequently suffer from imperfect capture conditions, making degradation resilience a critical deployment dependency. Implementing degradation-aware training, as validated by SpaceDG, is an essential engineering action to ensure MLLMs perform reliably in real-world AEC workflows. This mitigates a major failure mode where models trained on clean data fail catastrophically on noisy inputs, ensuring robust spatial intelligence across diverse input qualities.

## Decomposing Visual Hierarchies and Auditing Cross-Modal Synergy: Lessons from COCOTree, FastTab, and VLM Explainability

For AEC applications, a comprehensive understanding of documents extends beyond basic recognition to include the decomposition of complex visual hierarchies and the ability to audit MLLM reasoning for trustworthiness. COCOTree offers a novel approach to open tree-structured visual decomposition, a capability that is plausibly transferable and highly relevant for understanding complex AEC documents like assembly drawings, electrical schematics, or layered architectural plans. This benchmark overcomes manual annotation bottlenecks through a fully automated generation pipeline that synergizes the semantic reasoning of Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) with the precise geometric grounding of SAM [E8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22068). The resulting COCOTree dataset is massive, featuring over 21,000 images and 1.8 million structural nodes, embracing an open-vocabulary space of over 3,multiple unique labels to capture the long-tail distribution of complex physical assemblies [E6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22068). A standardized evaluation protocol, the Open Tree Quality (OTQ) metric, jointly assesses mask precision, label accuracy, and structural consistency, with rigorous human evaluation confirming strong alignment with human structural judgment [E9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22068], [E10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22068).

Complementing this hierarchical understanding, specific structural parsing challenges like table recognition are critical in AEC. FastTab introduces an efficient table recognizer that achieves competitive structure recovery performance at low-latency inference across four benchmarks [E21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22422). Its grid-centric model combines a lightweight Tiny Recursive Module for global reasoning with axial 1D Transformer encoders to capture long-range dependencies, predicting row/column counts, headers, and separators to construct a grid and infer rowspan/colspan [E21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22422). This is a direct implication for AEC, where extracting data from tables in schedules, specifications, and reports is a high-value, latency-sensitive task.

Finally, for high-stakes AEC applications, auditing MLLM reasoning is paramount to ensure trustworthiness. A VLM explainability benchmark addresses this by introducing Synergistic Faithfulness (F_syn), a scalable metric rooted in the Shapley Interaction Index [E23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22168). This metric rigorously isolates the joint Harsanyi dividend between modalities, effectively decoupling visual plausibility from cross-modal faithfulness [E23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22168). This is critical because multimodal datasets often contain language priors and modality biases, leading VLMs to exhibit cross-modal redundancy where they might answer visual queries using text alone [E23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22168]. Evaluating 8 distinct XAI methods across 3 VLM architectures and 3 benchmark datasets revealed that explainers often over-index on visual salience and significantly underperform in capturing true cross-modal synergy [E24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22168). This poses a significant engineering challenge for AEC, where ensuring models genuinely leverage visual evidence for critical decisions (e.g., detecting discrepancies in a drawing) is essential for safety and reliability.

## From Research Benchmarks to AEC Production: Integrating Robust MLLM Capabilities into Autodesk Workflows

The advancements discussed offer concrete pathways for enhancing AEC foundation models and 2D document intelligence. Integrating these robust MLLM capabilities into production workflows requires a strategic, multi-faceted approach.

First, adopting new benchmarks is crucial for internal evaluation and validation. ReceiptBench's hierarchical sub-tasks [E5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22413) provide a granular framework for assessing document information extraction, while SpaceDG-Bench [E20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22536) is indispensable for quantifying and mitigating performance degradation under real-world visual noise. COCOTree's Open Tree Quality (OTQ) metric [E9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22068) offers a robust way to evaluate the decomposition of complex visual hierarchies, directly applicable to assembly drawings or layered schematics. These benchmarks should be integrated into our continuous integration/continuous deployment (CI/CD) pipelines for MLLM development.

Second, architectural enhancements are necessary. GeoWeaver's pre-reasoning geometric grounding framework [E11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558), which performs token-adaptive geometric evidence allocation [E15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558], [E13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558), should be explored for integration into our MLLM architectures. This is a direct implication for enhancing spatial reasoning on blueprints, ensuring models truly understand geometric relationships rather than merely recognizing shapes. This might involve a dedicated pre-processing module or a fine-tuning strategy that leverages GeoWeaver's principles.

Third, leveraging advanced structural parsing mechanisms is key. The Metric-Aware GRPO from ReceiptBench [E1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22413) offers a powerful method to enforce structural consistency, which is critical for parsing AEC schedules, forms, and bills of materials that demand precise formatting. Similarly, FastTab's low-latency table recognition [E21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22422) is directly applicable for efficient data extraction from tabular data within AEC documents, where speed and accuracy are paramount.

Finally, for high-stakes AEC applications, implementing explainability metrics like Synergistic Faithfulness [E23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22168) is a critical adoption test. This allows us to audit MLLM reasoning, ensuring models genuinely leverage cross-modal synergy rather than relying on unimodal biases, which is vital for building trust in AI-driven decisions in the built world.

A significant integration constraint and deployment dependency is the need for extensive domain-specific data and fine-tuning strategies. While these research artifacts provide foundational mechanisms, their optimal performance in AEC contexts will require adaptation to the unique visual language, terminology, and structural nuances of AEC documents, which often differ significantly from general-purpose datasets. This implies a continuous investment in data curation and specialized fine-tuning to achieve production-grade reliability and accuracy.

## Building Trustworthy AI for the Built World: Next Steps in MLLM Development

The journey towards truly intelligent AEC document processing hinges on developing MLLMs that are not only capable but also robust, reliable, and interpretable. The advancements in geometric grounding, structural understanding, and resilience to visual degradation are critical steps in this direction. GeoWeaver's token-adaptive geometric grounding [E15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22558) enables MLLMs to inherently grasp spatial relationships, while ReceiptBench's GRPO [E1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22413], [E5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22413) ensures structural consistency in information extraction. Furthermore, SpaceDG's degradation-aware training [E17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22536) directly addresses the pervasive issue of noisy real-world inputs, ensuring performance stability. The engineering implication is clear: we must integrate these mechanisms and adopt rigorous, domain-specific benchmarks, coupled with explainability tools, to audit and validate MLLM reasoning. This ongoing commitment to developing robust and interpretable AI is essential for fostering greater automation, intelligence, and trust in the built world.
